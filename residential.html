The vast majority of elevator.

The vast majority of elevator cabs are completely opaque, avoiding an effect of visual vertigo for passengers during the especially rapid vertical translation of large towers. However, for several years, the number of partially or almost completely glazed cabins has been gradually increasing, especially for offices, shops or access to station platforms, also making it possible to identify the location of the cabin. The cabin is sometimes an open nacelle accessible at the edge of the slab of each level for buildings having a hollow core of central circulation, without separation in the vision of vertical modes of movement, or can be located on the periphery and even on the facade overlooking outside.

indicates the position of the elevator.

It indicates the position of the elevator, as well as in most cases the direction. The first indicators were "analog", the numbers of the floors being first a dial with a needle, then aligned on the wall, and each number lit when the elevator passed there, as well as a luminous arrow which indicated the direction of travel.

Then came the digital LCD indicator, most often red under a black plate. The arrow was integrated into the display. In the 1980s came the LED indicator, which uses the same principle as the LCD indicator. Finally, the screens appear on several recent elevators. On these screens can also be indicated the date and time, or even a filmed sequence.
